Since we still have to approximate the gradient and we are only
  allowed 2 evaluations, the natural choice is to \textit{wiggle}
  all the parameters and compute the corresponding energies
  \(E([\theta_1 + c_k, \theta_2+c_k])\) and \(E([\theta_1 - c_k,
  \theta_2-c_k])\). Then the first term of the gradient can be
  computed as
\[\frac{\partial E(\vec{\theta_k})}{\partial \theta_{k1}} \approx
  \frac{E([\theta_1 + c_k, \theta_2+c_k]) -E([\theta_1 - c_k,
  \theta_2-c_k])}{2c_k}\]
and the second term of the gradient is
\[\frac{\partial E(\vec{\theta})}{\partial \theta_{k2}} \approx
  \frac{E([\theta_1 + c_k, \theta_2+c_k]) -E([\theta_1 - c_k,
  \theta_2-c_k])}{2c_k}\]
Note how both terms of gradient are equivalent, unlike the terms in
  FDGA. In that algorithm, we computed the partial derivatives by
  freezing the other parameters, which gave rise to the different
  terms in the gradient vector. Here, no such freezing occurs. While
  we significantly cut upon energy evaluations, it may seem that we
  get worse approximation to the gradient. But if we make a slight
  modification to our partial derivatives, it turns out that
  \textit{on average}, our gradient approximation is not that bad! In
  fact, SPSA reaches the same level of accuracy as FDGA for a given
  number of iterations.
The \textit{slight modification} to achieve such convergence is to
  denote the wiggles of parameters by random variables. Let
  \(\Delta_k = [\Delta_{k1}, \Delta_{k2}]\) be the wiggle vector
  where \(\Delta_{ki}\) is the wiggle of the \(i\)'th parameter. Then
  we can express the first component of the gradient as

   c_k\Delta_k)}{2c_k\Delta_{k1}}\]
